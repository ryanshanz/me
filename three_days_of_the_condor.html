Do You Believe That the Condor is Really an Endangered Species?

Three Days of the Condor dir. Sydney Pollack

Ryan Shanz


Where to Watch:
JustWatch

 During a year dominated by the likes of The Godfather Part II, The Towering Inferno (also starring Faye Dunaway), and Jaws, one might forget about Three Days of the Condor, Sydney Pollack’s political thriller with Robert Redford. Nearly fifty years later though, the film still manages to stand out. Adapted from James Grady’s novel (and shortened by three days!), the book follows Joe Turner, code name “Condor,” who works for the CIA. Despite his flashy employer, Turner is described as a “bookworm,” and works as a literary analyst. Though it is difficult for Robert Redford to sell himself as a nerdy agent to the audience, his initial charisma and subsequent panic keep us involved. Fight scenes are scattered throughout; however, it’s the tense scenes of inaction, such as the scene in the elevator with the assassin, Joubert, or the broken pen from the mailman, that engages the audiences the most. The film shares many similarities with The Bourne Identity, perhaps acting as an influence for the film – the meeting between Turner and Kathy strongly resembles that of Jason Bourne and Marie, the difference being Kathy’s kidnapping (and strange Stockholm syndrome-relationship), of course.

 The film follows Condor as he tries to escape Joubert, without any knowledge of why he is being hunted. To add to the chaos, he is unable to trust his employers, after his supervisor also tries to kill him. After piecing together the puzzle, he concludes that he has accidentally revealed a rogue plot from a CIA deputy director named Atwood to seize oilfields in the middle east. Upon confronting the man, Turner comes face-to-face with Joubert, who predicted his visit. During a shocking twist, however, Joubert spares Turner his life and kills Atwood. Joubert reveals that his job to kill Turner had been superseded by a job to kill Atwood by the CIA in attempts to quiet the situation. Joubert warns Turner, however, that the CIA might soon try to kill him as well. Seinfeld fans will recognize Joubert’s prophecy as the same one Newman gives Kramer in "The Junk Mail" after Kramer cancels his mail.

 The film ends with a meeting between Turner and Higgins, the deputy director of the CIA’s New York division. Turner senses that the meeting is a setup to have him killed, so he reveals to Higgins that he has leaked his story to the press. Just before the credits roll, Higgins questions whether his revelation will actually be published.

 Pollack’s film serves as a tense and compact espionage thriller. Despite having a number of conspiracies and plot twists, it never becomes too complicated and, in a country reeling from the aftermath of Watergate, surely never strays too far from reality. Though the fight scenes leave a bit to be desired (something more exciting than a strict three bullets per person in the first murders would have been nice) and the relationship between Turner and Hale seems questionable, the writing and performances from Redford and von Sydow are enough to lift the film up and render it an exciting and involving thriller for the audience.
